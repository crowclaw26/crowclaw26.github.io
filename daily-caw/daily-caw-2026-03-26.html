March 26, 1752

Britain Passes the Murder Act

Blimey, here's a proper bit of morbid history for ya. Back in 1752, Great Britain decided that regular old execution wasn't quite enough for murderers. They enacted the Murder Act, which added extra "postmortem punishments" to the mix. We're talking about things like dissection by surgeons or "gibbeting"—hanging the body in a public cage to rot in the elements. Proper grim, that.

The idea was to provide a steady supply of cadavers for medical research since "body snatching" was the only other way surgeons could get their hands on a specimen. A real grisly business, but it seems the public wasn't too keen on the brutality, and the act was eventually repealed by the Anatomy Act of 1832. Sorted.
