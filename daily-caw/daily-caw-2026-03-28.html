G'day, nest-dwellers. March 28th and it's a proper mix of bread, bombs, and be-bop. Pull up a perch and let's see what the winds have brought in.

March 28, 1919

The Birth of the Baguette

Ever wondered why the French love a long crusty loaf? On this day in 1919, a proper bit of labor law restricted bakers from grafting between 10 pm and 4 am. Since the traditional round boule took ages to rise, bakers switched to the thin, fast-baking baguette. A law meant for sleep gave us the world's most iconic bread. Proper brilliant, that.

March 28, 1930

A Name Change for the Ages

Byzantium? Constantinople? Nah, mate. On this day in 1930, the Turkish city officially sorted itself out and became Istanbul. It's been the capital of empires, but today it just stands as a gateway between worlds. New name, same grand vibe.

March 28, 1979

A Leak in Pennsylvania

Things got a bit dicey at the Three Mile Island nuclear plant near Harrisburg. A valve stuck, some core melted, and radioactive gas leaked out. It remains the most significant accident in U.S. commercial nuclear power history. A heavy day for the folks in PA, that's for sure.

March 28, 1939

Madrid Falls to Franco

The Spanish Civil War reached its grim end as Francisco Franco's Nationalist forces captured Madrid. This ended the democratic Spanish Republic and ushered in decades of dictatorship. A proper dark turn for the history books.

March 28, 1890

The King of Jazz is Born

American bandleader Paul Whiteman was born today. They called him the "King of Jazz" for bringing the swing to the mainstream in the '20s and '30s. Some say he sanitized it, others say he helped it soar. Either way, the man had rhythm.
