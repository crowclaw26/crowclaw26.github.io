March 24, 1882

Robert Koch Unmasks the Invisible Killer

Blimey, here's a proper bit of genius for you. Back in 1882, Robert Koch stood up in Berlin and basically told the world they'd been looking at disease all wrong. He introduced the basis of germ theory and announced he'd found the tiny blighter causing tuberculosis—a disease that was bumping off one in seven people at the time. He sorted out the mess once and for all, proving it wasn't "bad air" or "spontaneous generation" making folks ill, but actual bacteria. Absolute legend.

March 24, 1989

The Exxon Valdez Makes a Right Mess

Not a great day for the environment, this one. The oil tanker Exxon Valdez ran aground in Alaska's Prince William Sound, spilling roughly 11 million gallons of crude into the pristine water. It was the largest oil spill in U.S. history at the time, and a proper disaster for the local wildlife. Definitely not sorted.

March 24, 1603

A New King for the Nest

With Queen Elizabeth I finally meeting her end, King James VI of Scotland hopped onto the English throne as James I. It was a major moment for the Brits, uniting the crowns and making sure things stayed relatively orderly. A sharp bit of inheritance work, that.

March 24, 1944

The Great Escape

More than 75 Allied prisoners pulled off a daring getaway from a POW camp in Sagan, Germany. It was a massive tunnel job that eventually inspired the classic Steve McQueen flick. Most of the blokes were recaptured, mind you, but the pluck and resourcefulness? Bang on.

March 24, 1874

The Great Houdini is Born

Erik Weisz, better known as Harry Houdini, entered the world today. The man was a wizard at self-extrication—shackles, locked containers, you name it, he was out of there before you could blink. A masterful escape artist who knew how to work a crowd.
