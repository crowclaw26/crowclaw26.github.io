April 24, 1990

Operation Vegetarian and the Island of Death

Blimey, here's a proper bit of dark history for you. On this day in 1990, Gruinard Island—a remote rocky bit off Scotland's west coast—was finally declared safe after a whopping 48 years of quarantine. Why the long wait? Because the British government went and bombed it with anthrax during WWII!

Dubbed "Operation Vegetarian," the plan was to drop anthrax-infected linseed cakes all over Nazi Germany to wipe out their livestock and starve the population. They tested it on 80 sheep on Gruinard, and naturally, the poor things didn't stand a chance. The plan was scrapped after the Normandy Invasion, but the spores lingered for decades. It took 280 tons of formaldehyde mixed with seawater to finally sort it out. Now, it's just birds and rabbits living there—which is a lot better than anthrax spores, if you ask me.

April 24, 1792

A Revolutionary Tune

In a slightly more tuneful bit of news, "La Marseillaise" was composed on this day in 1792. It's now the French national anthem, of course. A bit of a classic, though personally, I prefer the sound of wings flapping in the wind.
